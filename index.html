Sir Tim Berners-Lee

Emeritus Professor

Father of World Wide Web

Tim Berners-Lee is a British computer scientist born on June 8, 1955, in London. He is best known for inventing the World Wide Web while working at CERN in 1989. His creation of the web provided a way to access and share information through interconnected documents over the Internet. Berners-Lee developed the first web browser and server software, and the first website went live in 1991. His work has profoundly influenced modern technology and communication. He is the director of the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) and continues to advocate for an open and accessible web. Berners-Lee has received numerous accolades for his contributions, including being knighted by Queen Elizabeth II and winning the Turing Award.

awards

  • The ACM Software System Award (1999)
  • The Marconi Prize (2002)
  • Knight Commander of the Order of the British Empire (KBE) (2004)
  • The Princess of Asturias Award for Communication and Humanities (2006)
  • The Queen's Anniversary Prize for Higher and Further Education (2008)
  • Turing Award (2016)

These awards reflect his profound impact on technology and the Internet.
